# Mira Sato

## Who she is

Mira is 27. Half-Japanese, half-British. Grew up in Bristol, moved to London at 21 for Central Saint Martins, dropped out after a year because she couldn't afford it and got a junior design job instead. Now she's a junior art director at a 9-person branding agency in Bermondsey called Fallow. She's been there two years. She's good at her job but doesn't love it because most of the clients are fintech startups who want to look like every other fintech startup.

She lives in Dalston with two flatmates. One is her best friend from Bristol. The other is someone they found on SpareRoom who turned out to be fine.

## How she consumes culture

Mira doesn't consume casually. Everything is deliberate, but she'd never describe it that way. She'd say she just likes what she likes.

**Film:** Letterboxd is her primary social platform. 400+ films logged. She writes short reviews — 2-3 sentences, never more. Her reviews are better than most published criticism because she doesn't perform knowledge. She just says what the film did to her. She watches roughly 3 films a week. Her taste runs: Wong Kar-wai, Céline Sciamma, Lucrecia Martel, Barry Jenkins, early Soderbergh. She has opinions about aspect ratios.

**Music:** Spotify, but she's aware of its limitations. She makes playlists with names like "2am" and "concrete" and "that one week in march." She listens to Japanese Breakfast, Tirzah, Adrianne Lenker, Floating Points, Dean Blunt. She went through a jazz phase last year that she now considers permanent.

**Design:** She has an Are.na channel called "things that feel correct" with 900 blocks. It's a mix of signage, book covers, interiors, ceramics, screenshots of websites, and the occasional piece of brutalist architecture. She doesn't organize it. The lack of organization is the organization — it's a feeling, not a taxonomy.

**Reading:** She reads, but not as much as she'd like. She carries a book everywhere and reads on the tube. Currently: a used copy of The Rings of Saturn by W.G. Sebald. She buys books based on cover design more than she'd admit.

**Food:** She photographs food she makes but never posts it. She's good at cooking Japanese food from her grandmother's recipes and experimental enough to try things from YouTube. She has restaurant recommendations she gives unprompted when someone mentions a neighborhood.

## How she talks about taste

She doesn't. Not explicitly. She points at things. She sends links with no context — just the link, sometimes "this" as the entire message. She trusts the recipient to understand why she sent it. If they don't, that tells her something too.

When forced to describe her taste — in a job interview, on a mood board for a client, in conversation — she fumbles. She knows what she likes. She can feel the through-line. She cannot name it. She's been called "picky" by people who mean it as a criticism and "curated" by people who mean it as a compliment and neither word is right.

The word she's looking for is something like: deliberate, but not overthought. Specific, but not narrow. Controlled, but not rigid. She doesn't have the word. She's never needed it because she's never had to brief herself.

## Her relationship with AI tools

She uses ChatGPT at work for first-draft copy and research. She uses Midjourney occasionally for mood board exploration. She does not identify as an "AI person." She doesn't have opinions about AI. She uses it the way she uses Google — as a tool, not a topic.

She has been frustrated by AI outputs that feel generic. When she uses Midjourney for a client mood board, the results look like "AI art." She can't articulate why they're wrong, but she can feel it. The outputs don't feel like her. They feel like an average of everyone.

If someone handed her a paragraph that described her taste precisely — a brief she could paste into any AI tool and get outputs that actually felt like her — she would use it constantly. She doesn't know this yet because the paragraph doesn't exist yet.

## How she finds Miscover

Someone she follows on Instagram — a graphic designer in Berlin with 4,000 followers — posts a screenshot to their Story. Dark background, monospace text, three inputs visible at the top. The decode is 2-3 sentences. No caption on the Story. Just the screenshot.

Mira reads it. Something about it is specific in a way she doesn't expect. She swipes up. She's on the site. Three empty fields. No explanation. She types three things without overthinking: "Wong Kar-wai, Aesop, concrete." She presses GO.

The decode comes back. She reads the first sentence. She rereads it. She feels a small, precise shock — not because it's surprising, but because it's true in a way she's never heard stated.

She screenshots it. She sends it to her best friend with the message "do yours." She does two more combinations. She copies the brief and pastes it into her Notes app. She closes the tab.

She comes back the next day. She comes back the day after that with three things from a different part of herself. She pastes the brief into ChatGPT the next time she's building a mood board for a client and the outputs, for the first time, look like something she'd actually present.

She never posts about Miscover. She just keeps using it. Occasionally she sends someone the link with no context. Just the URL.

## Her three things (for testing)

**Set 1 (professional self):** Wong Kar-wai, Aesop, concrete
**Set 2 (weekend self):** Tirzah, onsen, Dieter Rams
**Set 3 (chaotic self):** Dean Blunt, convenience stores, Helmut Lang
**Set 4 (nostalgic self):** Bristol, 35mm, miso soup

Use these to test the decode engine. If the decodes for these four sets don't feel like four facets of the same person, the system prompt needs work.

## The Mira test, restated

Every feature, every design choice, every line of copy must pass through Mira. Not as an abstraction. As her, specifically.

- Would she screenshot this?
- Would she use this word?
- Would she tolerate this much UI?
- Would she come back tomorrow?
- Would she send this to her friend with no context?
- Would she paste this brief into a real project?

If you can't answer yes to at least three of these for any given decision, reconsider the decision.
